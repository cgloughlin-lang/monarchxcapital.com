What PE Firms Miss in Marketing Due Diligence

Christopher Loughlin · 10 min read

Private equity firms have become sophisticated at financial diligence, operational diligence, and even technology diligence. But marketing and commercial due diligence remains a persistent blind spot — one that regularly costs PE sponsors millions in post-close value destruction.

The problem isn't that PE firms ignore marketing. Most deal teams include some form of commercial assessment in their diligence process. The problem is that conventional commercial diligence is conducted by financial analysts and strategy consultants who evaluate marketing the way they evaluate every other business function: through spreadsheets, benchmarks, and surface-level interviews.

Marketing due diligence requires operator-grade assessment — someone who has built and run commercial engines, who can distinguish between a marketing function that's creating enterprise value and one that's burning cash while generating impressive-looking vanity metrics.

The Cost of Incomplete Marketing Diligence

When marketing diligence is inadequate, the consequences manifest 6-18 months post-close — precisely when the value creation plan should be accelerating. Common outcomes include:

Pipeline collapse. The target company's pipeline was dependent on the founder's network, a single channel, or unsustainable spend levels that were inflated to present well during the sale process. Post-close, when spend normalizes or the founder transitions out, pipeline drops 30-50%.

Customer acquisition economics don't scale. The target's customer acquisition cost (CAC) looked reasonable at current scale, but the channels that produced those economics are saturated. Scaling requires new channels with fundamentally different economics — a discovery that should have been made during diligence, not during the first board meeting post-close.

Brand equity is thinner than reported. The target claims strong brand awareness and market position, but diligence didn't validate these claims with independent research. Post-close, the brand turns out to be known within a narrow network but invisible in the broader market.

Marketing team capability gaps. The marketing team was capable at the target's current scale but doesn't have the skills, systems, or leadership to execute the value creation plan. This requires an immediate leadership upgrade that should have been budgeted pre-close.

Competitive collision. The target's market position is more contested than the competitive landscape analysis suggested. A larger or better-funded competitor is pursuing the same segment, and the target's differentiation isn't strong enough to defend market share under competitive pressure.

The Six Dimensions of Operator-Grade Marketing Diligence

Effective marketing due diligence goes beyond the standard questions about marketing spend, headcount, and agency relationships. It should assess six dimensions that determine whether the marketing function will accelerate or impede value creation:

1. Revenue Attribution Architecture

The single most important question in marketing diligence is: can the company credibly attribute revenue to specific marketing activities? Not in aggregate ("marketing contributed 40% of pipeline") but specifically — which channels, campaigns, and activities produced which pipeline, at what cost, with what conversion rate?

What to look for:

  • Multi-touch attribution model that the team actually uses for decisions
  • Consistent tracking from first touch through close, integrated with the CRM
  • Historical data that shows trends, not just current snapshots
  • Ability to isolate organic growth from paid growth from inherited pipeline

Red flags:

  • Attribution based on last-touch only (overweights bottom-of-funnel activities)
  • "Marketing pipeline" defined differently by marketing and sales teams
  • No attribution at all — "we know marketing works but can't prove which parts"
  • Attribution data that contradicts financial data (marketing claims $50M in pipeline contribution, but the company only closed $40M total)

2. Channel Dependency and Scalability

How concentrated is the company's customer acquisition across channels? And can the current channels scale to support the value creation plan?

Many PE targets have significant channel concentration risk — 60-80% of new business coming from a single channel (often the founder's network, a single partner, or paid search). This concentration may be acceptable at current scale but becomes a critical vulnerability when the value creation plan calls for 2-3x revenue growth.

Assess each channel across three dimensions: current contribution (percentage of pipeline and revenue), marginal economics (does CAC increase or decrease as you invest more?), and ceiling (how much total pipeline can this channel realistically produce?).

The value creation plan's revenue targets should be mapped against channel capacity. If the plan calls for $30M in new revenue and the current channels can produce $15M at acceptable economics, you need to identify and validate the channels that will produce the other $15M — during diligence, not post-close.

3. Competitive Positioning and Collision Risk

Standard competitive analysis catalogs competitors and compares features. Operator-grade diligence assesses competitive collision — where the target and its competitors are actively fighting for the same customers, in the same channels, with similar value propositions.

Collision analysis examines:

  • Segment overlap. Are competitors pursuing the same customer segments? How frequently does the target encounter competition in deals?
  • Channel overlap. Are competitors advertising on the same keywords, attending the same events, using the same partners? What happens to economics when multiple players compete in the same channel?
  • Positioning overlap. Can customers articulate why they would choose the target over alternatives? Or do they perceive the target as interchangeable with competitors?
  • Investment asymmetry. Are any competitors significantly out-investing the target in marketing, product, or sales? Investment asymmetry in early or growth-stage markets is a leading indicator of future market share shifts.

The output of collision analysis should directly inform the value creation plan. If collision risk is high in the target's current segments, the plan may need to include segment expansion, product differentiation, or defensive marketing investments that weren't in the original model.

4. Content and Thought Leadership Asset Audit

In B2B markets, content and thought leadership assets are critical infrastructure for pipeline generation, sales enablement, and competitive differentiation. Diligence should assess the quality, quantity, and strategic alignment of these assets.

Key questions:

  • Does the company have a content library that supports the full buyer journey — from awareness through evaluation through decision?
  • Is the content genuinely differentiated, or is it generic industry content with the company's logo?
  • What's the organic search position for commercially important keywords? Are content assets driving organic traffic, or is the company entirely dependent on paid channels?
  • Does the sales team actually use the content in their selling process, or do they create their own materials?

Content deficits are common in PE-backed turnarounds. Building a credible content library from scratch takes 12-18 months — that's time and investment that should be in the post-close plan if the assessment reveals gaps.

5. Marketing Technology and Data Infrastructure

The marketing technology stack is both an asset and a liability. Diligence should assess not just what technology exists, but whether it's integrated, utilized, and producing actionable data.

Common findings that impact value creation:

Tool sprawl with poor integration. The company has 15 marketing tools that don't talk to each other, producing fragmented data and requiring manual reconciliation. Rationalizing the tech stack is a 6-month project that competes with revenue-generating activities.

CRM data quality. The CRM is the foundation of commercial operations. If it's full of duplicate records, incomplete data, and inconsistent definitions, every downstream commercial activity — targeting, pipeline management, forecasting, reporting — is compromised.

Analytics capability. Can the marketing team produce the reporting that the PE sponsor will require? Board-quality dashboards showing pipeline metrics, channel performance, and commercial KPIs? Or will this require new tools, new skills, or new hires?

6. Team and Organizational Assessment

The final dimension is the most sensitive and the most impactful. Can the existing marketing team execute the value creation plan?

Assess along several axes:

Leadership capability. Does the marketing leader have experience operating at the scale the value creation plan envisions? Leading marketing at a $50M company is fundamentally different from leading marketing at a $200M company. If the plan calls for 3x growth, has the leader demonstrated that capability before?

Functional coverage. Does the team have the right skills — demand generation, product marketing, content, analytics, operations — to execute the plan? Most mid-market marketing teams have significant functional gaps.

Agency dependency. What percentage of marketing execution is outsourced to agencies? High agency dependency can work, but it creates cost structure, quality control, and institutional knowledge risks. Understand whether the agency relationships are strategic or a substitute for missing internal capability.

Retention risk. Are key marketing team members at risk of leaving post-close? In change-of-control situations, marketing leaders are among the most likely to depart — and their departure can create the same GTM disruption that leadership transitions always create.

Building the Diligence Into the Value Creation Plan

The point of marketing diligence isn't to kill deals — it's to price risk accurately and build realistic value creation plans. Every finding from the diligence should translate into one of three plan adjustments:

Investment adjustment. The marketing function needs more (or different) investment than the base model assumes. This could be technology, talent, content, or channel spend.

Timeline adjustment. The commercial acceleration assumed in the value creation plan will take longer than modeled. Channel development, team building, and market positioning take time that financial models often underestimate.

Intervention planning. Specific issues identified in diligence need to be addressed in the first 100 days post-close. This might mean bringing in an embedded operator to rebuild the commercial function, hiring new marketing leadership, or restructuring the marketing organization.

The deals that destroy the most value aren't the ones with bad marketing. They're the ones where nobody checked.

Marketing due diligence isn't optional in today's deal environment. With valuations elevated and the margin for operational error compressed, PE firms that conduct operator-grade commercial diligence will consistently outperform those that treat marketing as a line item rather than a value creation lever.

The investment in rigorous marketing diligence is trivial compared to the cost of discovering commercial problems 12 months post-close. Make it a standard part of every deal process.
